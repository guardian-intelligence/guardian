# TigerBeetle financial model

This document is the production contract for Guardian's operational billing
ledger. It fixes the asset scale, ledger IDs, account and transfer codes,
balance constraints, credit-allocation policy, two-phase transfer lifecycle,
correction rules, and payment-processor reconciliation model.

TigerBeetle owns accepted balances, reservations, and balance-changing facts.
PostgreSQL owns the catalog, commercial policy, business identifiers, and
customer documents. Stripe is a payment rail. ClickHouse is evidence and a read
model. None of those systems may calculate an alternative spendable balance.

This is an operational credit ledger, not Guardian's GAAP general ledger.
Finance exports map its immutable movements and billing documents into the
general ledger without putting financial reporting policy on the sandbox
admission path.

## System boundary

PostgreSQL owns:

- products, credit buckets, SKUs, and versioned rate cards;
- organizations and nonzero numeric ledger-party IDs;
- grant source, scope, validity interval, and commercial description;
- plans, contracts, phases, billing cycles, and entitlement materialization;
- customer-specific rates, discounts, and overage policy;
- billing windows and the TigerBeetle IDs assigned to each ledger leg;
- provider objects and events, customer documents, taxes, and corrections; and
- durable command state and reconciliation cursors.

TigerBeetle owns:

- posted and pending balances;
- atomic enforcement that a credit balance cannot be overspent;
- grant issuance, usage reservations, settlement, voids, and expiry;
- payment, fee, refund, dispute, payout, and receivable movements; and
- the immutable correction trail.

The gateway must synchronously reach TigerBeetle before admitting billable
work. A PostgreSQL row may describe an intended reservation, but it is not a
hold and must never be subtracted from a balance as though it were one.
TigerBeetle `debits_pending` is the only reservation authority.

SKU and bucket scope are deliberately not TigerBeetle account or ledger types.
A scoped grant has one TigerBeetle customer-credit account and a PostgreSQL
row that says where it may be spent. PostgreSQL selects eligible accounts;
TigerBeetle atomically reserves the selected amounts.

## Asset and ledger registry

Guardian represents USD value at asset scale 12:

```text
1 ledger atom = USD 0.000000000001
1 cent        = 10_000_000_000 atoms
1 USD         = 1_000_000_000_000 atoms
```

Scale 12 lets every SKU use an integer price per millisecond with materially
more precision than a public rate card requires. Prices are authored as
`atoms_per_billable_unit`; duration SKUs use one millisecond as the billable
unit. A displayed per-minute price is derived from that integer, never the
other way around:

```text
charge_atoms = sum(quantity_i * duration_ms * atoms_per_unit_ms_i)
```

All arithmetic is checked unsigned 128-bit integer arithmetic. Floating point
is forbidden in pricing, reservation, settlement, document, and provider
conversion paths. Provider amounts in USD cents convert exactly by multiplying
by `10_000_000_000`.

The customer document total is rounded once, not once per window, to the
provider's currency exponent using round-half-to-even. The sub-cent residual is
an explicit `receivable_rounding` transfer and document line. Provider tax
amounts are explicit cent-denominated lines; they are not hidden in the
rounding residual.

Ledger IDs are immutable after the first production account is created:

| Ledger ID | Name | Asset | Scale | Use |
| ---: | --- | --- | ---: | --- |
| `1` | `guardian_usd_v1` | USD-denominated Guardian credit | `12` | Production customer and provider movements |
| `2` | `guardian_usd_synthetic_v1` | Synthetic USD-denominated Guardian credit | `12` | Production canary and destructive lifecycle tests |

The synthetic ledger uses the same account and transfer codes so it exercises
the production model, but it never contains a live-mode provider object.
Stripe test-mode events and synthetic provider accounts may use ledger `2`;
they must never enter ledger `1`.

There is no ledger per organization, product, bucket, SKU, plan, or grant
source. A new currency or an incompatible future asset scale requires a new
reviewed ledger ID. Currency conversion uses two linked transfers, each
between a same-ledger account pair for one of the assets; values are never
silently normalized into USD.

## Identity and indexed metadata

Public and domain IDs remain PostgreSQL IDs such as `grant_id`, `window_id`,
`cycle_id`, `document_id`, and `provider_event_id`. TigerBeetle account,
transfer, and correlation IDs are separate 128-bit time-based IDs generated by
the TigerBeetle client and persisted before submission. A retry reuses the
exact ID and payload. IDs are not hashes of domain IDs and do not encode a
code, leg number, organization, or timestamp.

No personal data enters TigerBeetle. The indexed fields have one convention:

| Object | `user_data_128` | `user_data_64` | `user_data_32` |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Customer account | Persisted correlation ID for its PostgreSQL grant or receivable | Organization ledger-party ID | Product registry ID, or `0` for account-wide |
| Operator account | Persisted operator-account registry ID | Provider or campaign registry ID, when applicable | Product registry ID, or `0` |
| Usage transfer | Billing-window correlation ID | Organization ledger-party ID | Metered SKU registry ID |
| Grant transfer | Grant correlation ID | Organization ledger-party ID | Product registry ID, or `0` |
| Provider transfer | Provider balance-transaction correlation ID | Provider merchant-account registry ID | Provider registry ID |
| Document/correction transfer | Document or correction correlation ID | Organization ledger-party ID | Product registry ID, or `0` |

PostgreSQL is the index from these numeric values to business objects and text
identifiers.

Stripe can make those signed balances cross zero through fees, refunds,
reversals, reserves, or disputes. External facts must always be recordable;
they must not be rejected to preserve an internal assumption.

## Grants and allocation

Every grant creates exactly one `customer_credit` account and exactly one
issuance transfer. Its PostgreSQL row records:

- source: `free`, `contract`, `promotion`, `goodwill`, or `purchase`;
- scope: `organization`, `product`, `bucket`, or `sku`;
- `not_before` and optional `expires_at`;
- original amount in atoms;
- rate-card and entitlement-line provenance; and
- TigerBeetle account, issuance-transfer, and correlation IDs.

For example, a "$40 of Premium NVMe Storage" grant is a PostgreSQL grant with
scope `sku = premium_nvme_storage`, amount `40_000_000_000_000`, and a
TigerBeetle `customer_credit` account. TigerBeetle does not need to understand
that Premium NVMe belongs to the Storage bucket. An account-wide $40 grant is
the same ledger shape with `scope = organization`.

Purchased top-ups are organization-wide by default, do not expire by default,
and are not overage consent. Jurisdiction-specific expiry or escheat policy
must be explicit PostgreSQL policy before a purchased grant is issued.

For one requested SKU, eligible grants are ordered deterministically:

1. included allowance: free, then contract;
2. promotion, then goodwill;
3. purchased credit;
4. within a source class, narrowest eligible scope first: SKU, bucket,
   product, organization;
5. earliest expiry, then earliest creation, then account ID.

This consumes included and expiring value before durable purchased value while
preserving narrowly usable credits within each source class. A reservation
persists the selected account IDs and exact leg order for later explanation.
Allocation runs independently for every SKU in a quantity vector; all
resulting legs still form one linked reservation chain. A broad grant may
therefore have several legs in one window, each tagged with a different SKU.

The balance shown for a grant is:

```text
posted = credits_posted - debits_posted
held   = debits_pending
available = credits_posted - debits_posted - debits_pending
```

The account constraint makes `available` nonnegative. A top-level customer
balance is a presentation only: the UI must retain scope and expiry, because
incompatible grants cannot be summed into one spendable number.

## Native pending-transfer lifecycle

### Reserve

Before a sandbox or renewable meter interval starts:

1. PostgreSQL resolves the immutable rate context and computes the maximum
   amount for the bounded interval in atoms.
2. It selects eligible grants and reads their TigerBeetle balances.
3. It generates and persists one TigerBeetle transfer ID per grant leg.
4. It submits the legs as one linked chain of `usage_charge` transfers with
   `flags.pending`, all with the same timeout policy and window correlation.
5. The caller may start work only when every leg is accepted.

A stale balance read is safe: a concurrent linked chain either wins first or
the later chain fails a `debits_must_not_exceed_credits` check atomically. The
gateway refreshes and retries the allocation a bounded number of times. It
never treats a failed chain as partial success.

The timeout is a leak breaker, not normal scheduling. It is measured in whole
seconds by TigerBeetle and must extend beyond the bounded work interval by the
declared settlement-repair budget. A billing window may not cross a grant
expiry, contract/cycle boundary, or rate-context boundary. Long-lived resources
renew through bounded windows before the current reservation expires.

### Settle

Settlement measures guest workload time in milliseconds. VM boot, image
preparation, scheduling, and teardown are not billable. The exact charge uses
the rate context captured at reservation.

The gateway resolves every pending leg in one linked chain:

- legs consumed in full use `post_pending_transfer` with `AMOUNT_MAX`;
- the final partially consumed leg posts the exact smaller amount, releasing
  its remainder; and
- wholly unused later legs use `void_pending_transfer`.

Each resolution has a new transfer ID and references the original transfer in
`pending_id`. A pending transfer resolves at most once. Partial posting is
terminal; its released remainder cannot later be posted.

If actual usage could exceed the reservation, the product must renew before
crossing the boundary. It may not create an unconstrained post-hoc charge. If a
platform defect lets work continue after the reservation expires or is
exhausted, Guardian records `usage_writeoff`, preserves the customer hard cap,
and pages on the defect.

### Void and expire

Launch failure or cancellation explicitly voids every leg in one linked chain.
No service-layer balance update accompanies the void.

Expiration restores the pending amount automatically and cannot later be
posted or voided. Reconciliation looks up the original transfer before
deciding whether to post or void. An expired reservation is never inferred
from a local timer alone and never authorizes continued work.

At grant expiry, new reservations have already stopped and no reservation is
allowed to cross the boundary. After all pending legs resolve, a balancing
`grant_expire` transfer sweeps the remaining posted balance to
`expired_credit_sink`. In the same linked chain, a zero-amount
`grant_close` transfer with `pending | closing_debit` closes the account. It
has no timeout and intentionally remains pending; only an explicit
`void_pending_transfer` reopens the account. A grant with live ordinary
pending transfers is not closed.

## Prepurchase and payment allocation

Provider redirects and client callbacks never create credit. A top-up becomes
spendable only after a live provider event is durably recorded and the
TigerBeetle chain posts.

For a USD 40.00 Stripe top-up with a USD 1.46 fee, one linked TigerBeetle
command records:

1. `provider_payment_capture` for `40_000_000_000_000` atoms from
   `processor_settlement` to `processor_payment_clearing`;
2. `provider_fee_assess` for `1_460_000_000_000` atoms from
   `processor_fee_expense` to `processor_settlement`; and
3. `grant_issue_purchase` for `40_000_000_000_000` atoms from
   `processor_payment_clearing` to the new customer-credit account.

The processor-settlement debit balance rises by the provider's net USD 38.54;
the customer receives the full purchased USD 40.00. Duplicate provider events
reuse the same transfer IDs.

Customer-initiated refunds first create a pending `grant_refund_remove` so
unspent purchased value cannot be consumed while Stripe is processing the
refund. Provider success posts that pending transfer and links it to
`provider_refund`. Provider failure voids it. If an externally initiated
refund, dispute, or chargeback arrives after the credit was spent, the provider
movement is still recorded through `processor_dispute_expense`; Guardian never
forces a customer-credit account negative to make the provider state fit.
`grant_dispute_recovery` removes any still-unused affected credit and offsets
the provider loss only up to the recoverable amount.

## Plans, subscriptions, and contract changes

Stripe Subscriptions are not used. PostgreSQL owns plans, recurrence, contract
state, and billing cycles. A plan entitlement line specifies:

- grant scope and source;
- an allowance in atoms, or a quantity converted at a locked integer SKU rate;
- recurrence and expiry;
- an upgrade-family ID used for anti-arbitrage deltas; and
- the rate-card version applicable to the promised quantity.

At each period, entitlement materialization creates a new contract grant and
posts `grant_issue_contract`. A promise of `N` normal-storage minutes becomes
`N * 60_000 * normal_storage_atoms_per_ms`. A promise of `2N` premium-storage
minutes uses the independently locked premium rate. The original promised
quantity is retained in PostgreSQL for display and audit.

Free-tier grants are independent of paid contracts. Starting, changing,
canceling, or failing payment for a paid contract does not consume or close a
free grant.

Paid plan changes use these rules:

- **Upgrade:** effective immediately after payment or an explicit grace
  decision. Charge only the positive prorated recurring-price delta. Preserve
  existing grants until their original expiry and issue only the positive
  prorated allowance-value delta per upgrade family.
- **Anti-arbitrage:** compute the delta from value already issued for the
  period, not remaining balance. Spending before upgrading cannot increase the
  upgrade grant. Moving Hobby â†’ intermediate â†’ Pro at one effective timestamp
  issues the same total paid allowance value as Hobby â†’ Pro.
- **Cross-SKU upgrade:** value the already-issued normal and target premium
  allowances at their locked rates within the shared Storage upgrade family.
  The customer keeps the normal grant, while only the remaining target value
  is issued as premium credit.
- **Downgrade or cancellation:** effective at the next period boundary by
  default. Existing grants retain their original scope and expiry. No
  mid-cycle clawback or negative grant is created.
- **Terminal non-payment or fraud:** stop future materialization. After pending
  usage resolves, unused contract credit may move through `grant_revoke`.
  Consumed value is handled by a document, receivable, writeoff, or correction,
  never by mutating history.

These are one contract state machine for self-serve and enterprise agreements.
Enterprise recurrence, invoice collection, and negotiated rates are policy
fields, not a separate ledger model.

## Custom rates, promotions, and showback

Rate cards are immutable, versioned PostgreSQL rows. A reservation captures
the exact rate-card ID, customer override, integer unit rates, and billable
quantity vector. Settlement never rereads today's catalog price.

Commercial concessions remain explicit:

- a custom usage rate changes the captured integer SKU rate;
- a credit promotion issues a `grant_issue_promotion` balance;
- a recurring-price discount changes the billing-document line; and
- a goodwill correction issues a `grant_issue_goodwill` balance or a linked
  correction.

Those mechanisms are not interchangeable.

Guardian's dogfood organization uses the same SKUs, rate cards, pending
transfers, settlement, and documents as a customer. It receives contract or
promotional grants and an explicit 100% document discount where appropriate.
Usage still posts at the selected showback rate into `usage_sink`, so internal
cost by product, bucket, and SKU remains visible without a shadow billing path.

## Receivables and hard caps

A payment method on file is not overage consent. The default self-serve model
admits work only against posted grants and purchased credit, so TigerBeetle
enforces the hard cap directly.

An enterprise or future postpaid contract may accrue a `customer_receivable`
only under explicit contract authority. A pending `receivable_accrue` records
authorized but unsettled debt; posting it creates the receivable. Payment
capture moves through `processor_payment_clearing`, and `receivable_clear`
credits the customer-receivable account.

An upper postpaid credit limit is not implemented by a PostgreSQL subtraction.
Before offering postpaid overage, Guardian must add a reviewed TigerBeetle
balance-bound or separately funded authorization-account design and its
concurrency tests. Until then, contract grants are the approved way to express
a committed allowance or spend ceiling, and usage beyond them is denied.

## Corrections and immutable documents

Accounts and transfers are immutable. Corrections always add transfers:

1. void a still-pending erroneous transfer and create a new pending transfer;
2. reverse an erroneous posted movement with `correction_reverse`;
3. apply the intended movement with `correction_apply`; and
4. link reversal and replacement when they must succeed atomically.

Correction transfers use new IDs and the original operation's
`user_data_128` correlation. PostgreSQL records the reason, actor, source
document, and approval. An incorrect correction is corrected by another
transfer.

If a reversal would violate a customer-credit or clearing-account balance
constraint because value was already consumed, the reversal is not forced and
the constraint is not disabled. The difference becomes an explicit
receivable, goodwill grant, provider-dispute expense, or writeoff according to
the approved policy.

Issued billing documents are immutable. Price, tax, provider, and usage
corrections produce a credit note, debit note, or adjustment document linked
to the original. The document and its ledger correction share a correlation
but retain distinct domain and TigerBeetle IDs.

## Reconciliation

### PostgreSQL, journal, and TigerBeetle

Continuous reconciliation proves:

1. every registered account exists with the exact ledger, code, flags, and
   indexed metadata;
2. every posted grant has exactly one matching account and issuance transfer;
3. every reserved window leg is a live TigerBeetle pending transfer, and every
   TigerBeetle usage hold maps to one known window leg;
4. every settled or voided leg has exactly one matching resolution with the
   expected `pending_id`, amount, code, and flags;
5. deposits, usage, expiry, revocation, refunds, receivables, and corrections
   reconstruct each account's four TigerBeetle balance fields;
6. due command envelopes are drained and the off-site recovery journal agrees
   with accepted TigerBeetle objects; and
7. included debits and credits net to zero for each ledger.

Unknown accounts or transfers, field mismatches on an existing ID, an
unexplained pending transfer, or an accounting-identity failure is critical
drift. It trips the affected ledger's write guard and pages. Repair replays the
same immutable command or posts an explicit correction; it never edits
TigerBeetle history or synthesizes a balance from PostgreSQL.

### Stripe and other processors

Webhooks are low-latency notification, not the sole reconciliation source.
Every provider event is stored before application and deduplicated by provider
account plus event/object ID. Event order is not trusted.

For each provider merchant account and currency:

1. poll provider balance transactions with an overlapping cursor and persist
   the immutable gross, fee, net, currency, source object, availability time,
   and live/test mode;
2. map every balance transaction to one TigerBeetle correlation and the
   expected linked transfer group;
3. prove `gross - fee = net` after exact provider-unit conversion and compare
   direction, amount, and status;
4. match payouts and payout reversals to `provider_payout` movements;
5. prove the signed balance of `processor_settlement` equals the provider's
   opening balance plus activity minus payouts; and
6. run the provider's itemized Balance or Payout Reconciliation report as the
   daily close check after that report becomes available.

The provider comparison includes payments, refunds, fees, fee refunds,
disputes, dispute reversals, reserves, adjustments, payouts, and payout
failures. A previously unseen provider category is recorded but not
auto-classified; it blocks close and requires a new reviewed transfer mapping.

Near-real-time polling repairs missed webhooks. The daily provider report is
the authoritative close evidence. A monthly signed checkpoint records provider
opening and ending balances, TigerBeetle account balances, unmatched items,
and the journal high-water mark. No mismatch is "fixed" by changing a
provider object or overwriting a transfer.

## Required proofs

The financial-model readiness gate passes only when automated tests prove:

- exact scale conversion, checked multiplication, and half-even document
  rounding;
- duplicate account, transfer, provider event, and lost-reply retries;
- two concurrent reservations cannot overspend one grant;
- a multi-grant linked reservation is all-or-none;
- full post, partial post, explicit void, timeout, and expired-resolution
  behavior;
- a window cannot cross a grant, rate, or contract boundary;
- upgrade path independence across same-SKU and cross-SKU plans;
- purchased-credit refund before use, after partial use, and after provider
  success with an expired hold;
- correction failure when value is no longer reversible;
- provider gross, fee, net, refund, dispute, and payout reconciliation; and
- an isolated synthetic-ledger reconstruction from the off-site journal.
